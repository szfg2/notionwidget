The Causes of Hypoxia, from First Principles

Oxygen travels a single chain from atmosphere to mitochondrion. Hypoxia is a break anywhere along that chain — and the point of the break defines the type.

Clinical bottom line Don't memorise four categories — derive them. Tissue O₂ supply is one equation, DO₂ = CO × CaO₂, sitting downstream of alveolar loading. Walk the oxygen cascade and ask where it fails: at the alveolus (hypoxic), in the blood's carrying capacity (anaemic), in flow (ischaemic), or at the mitochondrion (histotoxic). The A–a gradient then splits the hypoxic causes into lung vs not-lung.

First, the word: hypoxaemia is not hypoxia

Hypoxaemia is a low partial pressure of O₂ in arterial blood. Hypoxia is an oxygen deficiency at the tissues — the thing that actually matters.[1] A patient can be profoundly hypoxic with a normal PaO₂ (cyanide, severe anaemia, shock) and conversely tolerate a low PaO₂ if delivery is otherwise preserved. The whole classification falls out of separating loading the blood from delivering and using the oxygen.

Tissue oxygenation is governed by global oxygen delivery — the master equation everything else hangs off:

\[ DO_2 = CO \times \big[\,(1.34 \times Hb \times S_aO_2) + (0.003 \times P_aO_2)\,\big] \]

Reads as: delivery is flow × content. Content is almost entirely haemoglobin-bound O₂; the dissolved term is trivial until you breathe high FiO₂. Three multiplicands, three ways to fail — flow, haemoglobin, saturation.

  • \(DO_2\) oxygen delivery (mL·min⁻¹); normal ≈ 1000 mL·min⁻¹
  • \(CO\) cardiac output (dL·min⁻¹ when content is per dL)
  • \(CaO_2\) arterial O₂ content — the bracketed term (mL·dL⁻¹)
  • \(1.34\) Hüfner's constant — mL O₂ bound per g Hb (theoretical 1.39; ~1.34 in vivo from COHb/MetHb)
  • \(Hb\) haemoglobin concentration (g·dL⁻¹)
  • \(S_aO_2\) arterial O₂ saturation (fraction)
  • \(0.003\) solubility of O₂ in plasma (mL·dL⁻¹·mmHg⁻¹)
CaO₂ = (1.34 × 15 × 0.98) + (0.003 × 100) = 19.7 + 0.3 ≈ 20 mL·dL⁻¹. Note the dissolved O₂ contributes ~1.5% — anaemia and desaturation hit content hard; raising PaO₂ alone barely moves it.

Walk the cascade: four break-points, four types

Trace one O₂ molecule from inspired gas to the electron transport chain. Each handover is a place the chain can break, and each break has a name. This is the entire taxonomy in one line.[1][2]

Hypoxic hypoxia: use the A–a gradient to localise the lesion

Hypoxic hypoxia (hypoxaemia) is a low PaO₂, conventionally PaO₂ < 60 mmHg.[1] The single most useful next step is the A–a gradient, because it asks one question: is the alveolus delivering O₂ to the blood, or not? A normal gradient means the lung is innocent — the problem is the gas reaching the alveolus or the ventilation moving it. A widened gradient means the lung itself is the lesion.

The alveolar gas equation sets what the alveolus offers

\[ P_AO_2 = F_iO_2\,(P_{atm} - P_{H_2O}) - \dfrac{P_aCO_2}{R} \]

Reads as: the alveolus offers inspired O₂ minus the space CO₂ takes up. Two terms, two normal-gradient mechanisms — drop the first (low FiO₂ / low Patm) or inflate the CO₂ term (hypoventilation).

  • \(F_iO_2\) inspired O₂ fraction (0.21 room air)
  • \(P_{atm}\) atmospheric pressure (760 mmHg at sea level)
  • \(P_{H_2O}\) saturated water vapour pressure (47 mmHg at 37 °C)
  • \(P_aCO_2\) arterial CO₂ tension (mmHg) — the hypoventilation lever
  • \(R\) respiratory quotient (≈ 0.8)
Room air, sea level, PaCO₂ 40: PAO₂ = 0.21 × (760 − 47) − 40/0.8 ≈ 150 − 50 = 100 mmHg.

The gradient itself

\[ A\text{–}a = P_AO_2 - P_aO_2 \]

Reads as: what the alveolus offers minus what the artery delivers. Normal is roughly (age/4) + 4 mmHg on room air — a widened gap localises the fault to gas transfer across the membrane (diffusion or shunt/V̇Q̇).

The 100% O₂ response is the bedside discriminator: a true shunt resists supplemental O₂ because the shunted blood never meets alveolar gas, and the already-saturated non-shunt blood can't add meaningful content on the flat top of the dissociation curve.[1]

Normal-gradient hypoxaemia: the pump, not the lung

When the gradient is normal, the gas exchanger works — the problem is upstream. Either the inspired O₂ is low, or alveolar ventilation has fallen and CO₂ has displaced alveolar O₂. Hypoventilation is best worked through anatomically, from brainstem to bellows — a single lesion anywhere in the respiratory pump produces the same gas picture.[1]

Anaemic hypoxia: content collapses while PaO₂ lies normal

Here the trap is the blood gas. PaO₂ and SaO₂ can look perfect while content — the quantity that feeds DO₂ — has fallen, because content is dominated by the Hb × SaO₂ product, not the dissolved term.[1][2] Two routes: too little haemoglobin (anaemia), or haemoglobin present but unusable.

Carbon monoxide is the instructive case. CO binds Hb with ~210–250× the affinity of O₂ and is released slowly, so it removes functional Hb from circulation — functionally an acute anaemia. Critically, the dissolved PaO₂ is unchanged, so the carotid bodies are not triggered and there is no compensatory hyperpnoea and pulse oximetry is falsely reassuring (it cannot distinguish COHb from O₂Hb).[1] CO also left-shifts the curve, further impairing tissue offloading.

Ischaemic and histotoxic: flow fails, then utilisation fails

Ischaemic (stagnant) hypoxia is the cardiac-output term of DO₂ collapsing — content per dL is fine but too few dL arrive per minute. Global (low CO/shock) or regional (vascular occlusion). Histotoxic hypoxia is the one type that lies entirely outside the delivery equation: DO₂ is normal, but the mitochondrion can't use the oxygen. Cyanide is the prototype, inhibiting cytochrome c oxidase (complex IV) and arresting oxidative phosphorylation — treated with methylene blue or nitrites, which generate methaemoglobin to scavenge cyanide as non-toxic cyanmethaemoglobin.[1][2]

Why rising demand alone rarely causes hypoxia

Increased VO₂ is usually buffered, not pathological — the body has large extraction reserve. It only tips into hypoxia when delivery is already compromised.[1]

\[ O_2ER = \dfrac{VO_2}{DO_2} = \dfrac{CaO_2 - CvO_2}{CaO_2} \approx \dfrac{250}{1000} = 0.25 \]

Reads as: at rest tissues extract only a quarter of what's delivered. Extraction can climb to ~70% before delivery becomes inadequate — that threshold is critical DO₂, below which VO₂ becomes supply-dependent and lactate rises.

  • \(VO_2\) oxygen consumption (≈ 250 mL·min⁻¹ at rest)
  • \(DO_2\) oxygen delivery (≈ 1000 mL·min⁻¹)
  • \(CvO_2\) mixed venous O₂ content (mL·dL⁻¹)
  • \(O_2ER\) extraction ratio; max ≈ 0.7, organ-dependent

This is why the end-product unifies all four types: once any mechanism pushes tissues past their extraction reserve, anaerobic metabolism generates lactate, producing a fixed-acid (high anion gap) metabolic acidosis with a base deficit. Hypoxia and the resulting acidosis then drive ventilation via the peripheral chemoreceptors, producing compensatory hypocapnia.[1]
