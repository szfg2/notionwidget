Shunt & venous admixture

Perfusion that never meets ventilation — why it drops PaO₂, spares PaCO₂, and refuses to budge with oxygen.

Clinical bottom line True shunt is perfusion of unventilated alveoli (V/Q = 0). PaO₂ falls in proportion to the shunt fraction and — unlike low-V/Q units — does not correct with supplemental O₂, because there is no ventilated gas for the added oxygen to reach. PaCO₂ is largely spared: ventilatory drive rises and the CO₂ dissociation curve is near-linear over the working range.

One spectrum, two failure modes

Every alveolar–capillary unit sits somewhere on a continuum of ventilation-to-perfusion ratios. Shunt and dead space are its two extremes: perfusion without ventilation at one end, ventilation without perfusion at the other.[2]

Normal physiological shunt is ~2–3% of cardiac output, which is why a healthy young adult's PaO₂ sits a few mmHg below the alveolar value rather than equal to it.[1]

You can't measure shunt — you model it

True shunt (V = 0) can't be separated from V/Q scatter (V/Q < 1) in a sample of left-heart blood; both lower arterial O₂ content. So we don't measure shunt directly — we compute venous admixture: the fraction of mixed venous blood that, if added to ideal pulmonary end-capillary blood, would reproduce the observed arterial O₂ content.[1][3]

\[ \frac{\dot{Q}_s}{\dot{Q}_t} = \frac{C_{c'}O_2 - C_aO_2}{C_{c'}O_2 - C_{\bar{v}}O_2} \]

Reads as: how big is the gap between ideal end-capillary blood and what actually reached the artery, scaled against the arterio–venous content difference. A larger numerator = more blood bypassing gas exchange.

  • Q̇s/Q̇t shunt fraction — shunt flow as a proportion of cardiac output
  • Cc′O2 pulmonary end-capillary O₂ content (mL/dL); assumed ideal, with Pc′O₂ = PAO₂
  • CaO2 arterial O₂ content (mL/dL), measured
  • Cv̄O2 mixed venous O₂ content (mL/dL), from a PA catheter sample

Because it lumps every cause of arterial desaturation into one notional V/Q = 0 compartment, the figure is a calculated equivalent, not an anatomical measurement — and it is FiO₂-dependent (see below).

Content uses CO₂ = (1.34 × Hb × SO₂) + (0.003 × PO₂). On 100% O₂, a persistent A–a gap that won't close points to true shunt rather than V/Q mismatch or hypoventilation.

Why true shunt ignores oxygen

This is the high-yield distinction. A low-V/Q unit still has some ventilation, so raising FiO₂ raises its PAO₂ and the blood leaving it oxygenates better. A true shunt unit has no ventilation — the inspired oxygen never contacts that blood, so PaO₂ barely responds however high you push FiO₂.[2] The isoshunt diagram makes the penalty visible: as shunt fraction climbs, the FiO₂–PaO₂ curve flattens.[4]

The CO₂ paradox

Intuitively, blood bypassing the lung should also retain CO₂ and raise PaCO₂. In practice, isolated shunt usually doesn't — for two reasons that both stem from the shape of the dissociation curves.[2]

1. Drive compensates. Any rise in PaCO₂ stimulates central chemoreceptors, alveolar ventilation increases, and the still-ventilated units blow off the extra CO₂. 2. The CO₂ curve is near-linear and steep. Over the physiological range, content rises almost linearly with tension, so well-ventilated units can offload enough CO₂ to offset the shunted blood. The O₂ curve, by contrast, is flat at the top — once end-capillary blood is saturated, extra ventilation adds almost no O₂ content, so it cannot compensate for the desaturated shunt fraction.

The asymmetry in one line Shunt drops PaO₂ but spares PaCO₂ because the CO₂ curve is steep where it matters and the O₂ curve is flat where it matters. PaCO₂ rises only when the shunt is large and the patient can no longer increase alveolar ventilation.
